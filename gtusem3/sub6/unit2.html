2.1 Concept, Typology, Genesis, and Growth of Medical Tourism

Concept of Health & Medical Tourism

Health Tourism is a broad term that includes all forms of travel where the primary purpose is to improve or maintain one’s health and well-being.

Medical Tourism, a subset of health tourism, refers to the practice of traveling to another country to obtain medical treatment. This may include:

  • Essential treatments: cardiac surgery, orthopaedic procedures, organ transplants
  • Elective procedures: dental work, cosmetic surgery
  • Reproductive care: IVF, surrogacy, fertility treatments

It combines medical expertise, affordable treatment, and travel/tourism appeal into a growing international industry.

Genesis and Historical Evolution

  • Ancient Period: Healing temples in Greece, hot springs in Rome, and Ayurveda in India were early examples of medical and wellness travel.
  • Medieval Period: Pilgrimages to sacred sites like Lourdes (France) for miraculous healing.
  • Modern Era:
    • 1980s–1990s: Globalization of healthcare began.
    • 2000s onwards: Surge in cross-border medical travel due to rising healthcare costs in Western countries and improved infrastructure in developing nations like India, Thailand, and Malaysia.

2.2 Benefits of Medical Tourism

To the Patients

  1. Cost-effective treatment: Surgeries in India can be 60–80% cheaper than in the US.
  2. High-quality services: Accredited hospitals with internationally trained doctors.
  3. No waiting time: Immediate access to surgeries and specialists.
  4. Combine treatment with travel: Recover in healing and scenic environments.
  5. Privacy and anonymity: Especially in cosmetic and fertility-related procedures.

To the Host Country

  1. Foreign exchange earnings: Boosts the economy.
  2. Healthcare infrastructure growth: Investment in hospitals and technology.
  3. Job creation: Healthcare, hospitality, and transport sectors benefit.
  4. Global recognition: Enhances the country's brand as a healthcare destination.

2.3 Factors Responsible for the Growth of Health & Medical Tourism

  1. Rising Healthcare Costs in Developed Countries
    Surgeries are expensive in the US, UK, and Canada.
    Example: Heart bypass surgery in the US costs approx. ₹45 lakh, while in India it may cost ₹2.5 lakh.
  2. Technological Advancements in Developing Nations
    Use of robotic surgeries, telemedicine, and advanced diagnostics in countries like India, Singapore, and South Korea.
  3. Globalization and Accessibility
    Easy visa norms, international flights, and digital access to hospitals. Medical travel facilitators and online consultation platforms simplify planning.
  4. Quality of Healthcare Professionals
    India, for instance, has highly qualified, English-speaking doctors trained internationally.
  5. Accreditation and Standardization
    JCI (Joint Commission International) and NABH (National Accreditation Board for Hospitals) ensure globally accepted care standards.
  6. Long Waiting Lists Abroad
    Patients in countries with nationalized healthcare (e.g., UK or Canada) face long waiting periods.
  7. Wellness Appeal and Traditional Systems
    India’s strength in Ayurveda, Yoga, Siddha, and Naturopathy attracts global wellness seekers.
