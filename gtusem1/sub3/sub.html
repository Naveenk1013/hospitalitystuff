1.1 Introduction to Culinary

  • Culinary refers to the art and science of preparing, cooking, presenting, and serving food.
  • It blends creativity with skills, techniques, and knowledge of ingredients.
  • Culinary professionals are not just cooks; they are artists and scientists in the kitchen.

1.2 Evolution of Global Food Culture

Food culture evolved over thousands of years, influenced by geography, climate, trade, migration, religion, and technology.

A. Ancient Times

  • Early humans ate raw fruits, nuts, and meat.
  • Discovery of fire (around 1.5 million years ago) ? cooking made food softer, tastier, and safer.
  • Different regions used local ingredients depending on geography.
  • Example: In cold regions, meat and fat; in tropical areas, fruits, grains, spices.

B. Influence of Civilizations

  • Egyptians & Greeks: Developed bread, wine, olive oil.
  • Romans: Spread cheese-making, sauces across Europe.
  • Chinese: Perfected rice, noodles, soy products.
  • Indian Civilization: Known for spices, Ayurveda-based cooking.
  • Example: Mediterranean diet still based on olive oil, bread, wine.

C. Age of Exploration & Trade (15th 18th Century)

  • Trade routes (Silk Route, Spice Route) spread food globally.
  • Columbian Exchange: Potatoes, tomatoes, maize from Americas; spices from Asia.
  • Colonial influence (British, Portuguese, French).
  • Example: Chili from Mexico to India via Portuguese ? now essential in Indian curries.

D. Industrial Revolution (18th 19th Century)

  • Invention of canning, refrigeration, transportation.
  • Mass production ? cheaper, widely available food.
  • Urbanization created demand for fast food.
  • Example: Rise of bread factories and railways made bakery products accessible.

E. Modern Globalization (20th 21st Century)

  • Air travel, TV, internet spread global cuisines.
  • Fusion food, fast food chains (McDonalds, KFC).
  • Focus on health foods, veganism, sustainability.
  • Example: Indian caf serving Italian pasta with masala or sushi with paneer.

1.3 Aims & Objectives of Cooking Food

  1. Make Food Safe: Destroys microorganisms, toxins. Example: Cooking chicken kills salmonella.
  2. Improve Digestibility: Breaks down fibers, starches. Example: Cooked rice is easily digested.
  3. Enhance Taste & Aroma: Develops flavors. Example: Roasting coffee beans.
  4. Improve Appearance & Texture: Adds color, shape. Example: Golden-brown paratha.
  5. Add Variety: Same ingredient ? different dishes. Example: Potato ? fries, curry, samosa.
  6. Retain Nutritional Value: Releases nutrients. Example: Steamed broccoli retains Vitamin C.
  7. Economize Food: Makes cheaper cuts palatable. Example: Leftover rice ? fried rice.
  8. Maintain Cultural Traditions: Reflects festivals, rituals. Example: Modak during Ganesh Chaturthi.

1.4 Attitudes and Behaviour in the Kitchen

  1. Discipline & Punctuality: Report on time, follow hierarchy. Example: Late arrival delays lunch service.
  2. Teamwork: Share responsibilities, support colleagues. Example: Banquet for 200 guests requires coordination.
  3. Respect for Food: Avoid wastage. Example: Neat peeling shows care.
  4. Cleanliness & Hygiene: Clean as you go. Example: Messy board causes contamination.
  5. Safety: Use equipment carefully. Example: Never run with knife.
  6. Positive Attitude: Accept feedback, stay calm. Example: Learn from mistakes.
  7. Professional Behaviour: Respect all, say Yes, Chef!

1.5 Personal Hygiene

Definition: Maintaining cleanliness of body and clothing to prevent food contamination.

Importance

  • Prevents foodborne diseases (Salmonella, E. coli).
  • Ensures food safety, quality, and customer trust.
  • Complies with HACCP, FSSAI.
  • Creates professional image.
  • Promotes staff health.
  • Prevents cross-contamination.

Key Practices

  1. Daily bath, short clean nails.
  2. Wash hands before/after handling food, toilet, sneezing.
  3. Clean uniform, cap, non-slip shoes.
  4. Hair tied and covered.
  5. No work when sick or injured.
  6. No smoking, chewing gum, eating while cooking.
  7. No jewelry.

1.7 Safety Procedure in Handling Equipment

  1. General: Follow instructions, unplug before cleaning. Example: Switch off mixer before removing jar.
  2. Knives: Right knife for task, cut on board, keep sharp. Example: Bread knife for bread.
  3. Electrical: Dry hands, no damaged wires. Example: Wet hand + microwave = shock.
  4. Gas: Check leaks, light match first. Example: Turn off if flame blows out.
  5. Heavy Equipment: Use guards, never put hands inside. Example: Use pusher in slicer.
  6. Cleaning: Cool before cleaning. Example: No cold water on hot tray.
  7. Fire Safety: Know extinguisher locations. Example: Use Class K for oil fires.

1. Introduction

Food commodities are the basic raw materials or ingredients used in food production and service. They include all food items that are purchased, stored, processed, cooked, and served in hotels, restaurants, and catering establishments. Understanding food commodities helps chefs and service professionals to ensure quality, nutrition, cost control, and menu planning.

2. Definition

A food commodity is any substance or product, whether raw, semi-processed, or processed, that is intended for human consumption. Example: Cereals, pulses, meats, fish, fruits, vegetables, dairy products, sugar, and spices.

3. Classification of Food Commodities

Category Description Examples
Perishable Commodities Items that spoil quickly and need refrigeration. Milk, meat, fish, fruits, vegetables, and eggs.
Non-Perishable Commodities Items that can be stored for a long time without spoiling. Rice, flour, sugar, salt, tea, coffee, and canned foods.
Semi-Perishable Commodities Items that have a moderate shelf life. Potatoes, onions, dried fruits, butter, and cheese.

4. Importance of Food Commodities in the Hospitality Industry

  1. Menu Planning: Helps in designing balanced and cost-effective menus.
  2. Cost Control: Knowledge of commodity prices and quality ensures profitable operations.
  3. Nutrition: Enables chefs to choose ingredients that provide balanced nutrition.
  4. Quality Assurance: Ensures the use of fresh, high-standard ingredients.
  5. Inventory Management: Helps maintain the right quantity and freshness of items.
  6. Guest Satisfaction: High-quality food commodities enhance taste and presentation.

5. Factors Affecting Selection of Food Commodities

Introduction: The selection of food commodities is a crucial part of food production and service in hotels and restaurants. Choosing the right ingredients ensures good quality, nutrition, taste, cost-effectiveness, and customer satisfaction. Several factors influence the selection of food commodities in the hospitality industry.

  1. Quality and Freshness: The most important factor. Only fresh, good-quality ingredients should be selected to ensure safe and tasty food. Example: Fresh fruits, crisp vegetables, and tender meat give better results than stale items.
  2. Availability and Seasonality: Some commodities are seasonal, such as mangoes or strawberries. Selection depends on what is available in the market during that time. Seasonal products are usually cheaper and better in quality.
  3. Cost and Budget: The price of commodities affects menu planning and food cost control. The goal is to select good-quality items within the budget to maintain profitability.
  4. Storage Facilities: Selection also depends on available storage space and equipment (cold rooms, refrigerators, dry stores). Perishable items should only be bought in small quantities if storage is limited.
  5. Supplier Reliability: Reliable suppliers provide consistent quality, timely delivery, and fair prices. Selection is easier when suppliers are trustworthy and meet the hotels standards.
  6. Menu Requirements: The choice of ingredients depends on the type of menu and dishes being prepared. Example: A continental menu requires different commodities compared to an Indian menu.
  7. Nutritional Value: Nutrient-rich food commodities are preferred to ensure a balanced and healthy diet. Especially important in hospitals, schools, and health-conscious restaurants.
  8. Shelf Life: Commodities with a longer shelf life (like rice, flour, or canned goods) are preferred for bulk buying. Perishable items (like milk or seafood) should be purchased daily.
  9. Legal and Hygiene Standards: Food commodities must meet FSSAI or local food safety regulations. Suppliers should follow proper hygiene and packaging standards.
  10. Customer Preferences: Selection should reflect the taste, dietary needs, and cultural preferences of customers. Example: Vegetarian guests, Jain cuisine, or gluten-free menu items.

Role of Fats and Oils Used in Cookery

1. Introduction

Fats and oils are essential ingredients in cookery that add flavor, texture, richness, and nutrition to food. They are used in various cooking methods such as frying, baking, roasting, and sauting. Both play a key role in energy supply, taste enhancement, and food preparation in the hospitality industry.

2. Definition

  • Fats are solid at room temperature (e.g., butter, ghee, margarine).
  • Oils are liquid at room temperature (e.g., sunflower oil, olive oil, mustard oil).

Both are composed mainly of fatty acids and glycerol, and serve as major sources of energy in the human diet.

3. Sources of Fats and Oils

Source Examples
Animal-based Fats Butter, Ghee, Fish Oil
Plant-based Oils Olive Oil, Mustard Oil, Sunflower Oil, Coconut Oil
Processed Fats Margarine, Shortening, Hydrogenated Fats

4. Role of Fats and Oils in Cookery

  1. Medium of Cooking: Used for frying, roasting, and sauting. Help transfer heat evenly and cook food quickly. Example: Deep-fried snacks, sauted vegetables.
  2. Enhances Flavor and Aroma: Adds richness and distinct taste to food. Some oils (like sesame or mustard) impart characteristic flavors to cuisines.
  3. Improves Texture and Mouthfeel: Makes food soft, moist, and smooth. Provides flakiness in pastries and crispness in fried items.
  4. Acts as a Tenderizer: Fats coat the flour particles and reduce gluten formation in dough. Makes cakes, pastries, and breads soft and tender.
  5. Used as a Spreading Agent: Butter, margarine, and ghee are used as spreads on bread, toast, and bakery items.
  6. Provides Moisture and Shine: Adds gloss and moisture to baked or roasted dishes. Helps retain freshness and prevents drying.
  7. Nutritional Value: Fats and oils are concentrated sources of energy (9 kcal per gram). They also provide fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, and K.
  8. Preservation: Used in pickles, chutneys, and canned foods to prevent spoilage by forming a protective layer.
  9. Conducts Flavor Compounds: Many herbs and spices dissolve in fat, enhancing their flavor release during cooking.

Health Considerations: Type of Fat Consumed

  1. Saturated Fats (Use in Moderation):
    • Found in butter, ghee, cream, lard, and red meat.
    • Can raise cholesterol levels and increase risk of heart disease if consumed in excess.
  2. Unsaturated Fats (Healthy Fats):
    • Found in vegetable oils (sunflower, olive, mustard, soybean).
    • Help lower bad cholesterol (LDL) and improve heart health.
  3. Trans Fats (Avoid):
    • Found in margarine, bakery products, and deep-fried fast foods.
    • Increase risk of obesity, diabetes, and cardiovascular diseases.

Spices and Herbs Used in Cooking

1. Introduction

Spices and herbs are essential ingredients used in cooking to enhance the flavor, aroma, and appearance of food. They are used in small quantities, but they play a major role in improving the taste, digestion, and nutritional value of dishes.

2. Definition

  • Spices: Dried parts of plants such as seeds, bark, roots, fruits, or flower buds used for flavoring food. Example: Clove, Cinnamon, Pepper, Cumin.
  • Herbs: The green leafy parts of plants used fresh or dried to flavor and garnish dishes. Example: Coriander leaves, Mint, Basil, Thyme.

3. Importance of Spices and Herbs in Cooking

  5. Natural Preservatives
  6. Enhance Nutritional Value
  7. Add Variety to Food
  8. Stimulate Appetite
  9. Cultural and Traditional Value
  10. Mask Undesirable Odors

Grains, Millets, Pulses, and Condiments in Cookery

A. Grains

Grains are the seeds of cereal plants that are rich in carbohydrates and form a major part of the human diet.

Examples: Rice, Wheat, Maize (Corn), Barley, Oats, Rye.

Uses: Rice (pulao, biryani), Wheat (chapati, bread), Maize (tortillas, popcorn).

Nutritional Value: Rich in carbohydrates and fiber.

B. Millets

Millets are small-seeded cereal grains that are highly nutritious and easy to grow in dry regions.

Examples: Ragi, Bajra, Jowar, Foxtail Millet, etc.

Uses: Porridge, rotis, dosa, khichdi.

Nutritional Value: Rich in protein, fiber, calcium, and iron. Gluten-free.

C. Pulses (Legumes)

Pulses are the dried edible seeds of leguminous plants, consumed as a source of protein.

Examples: Lentils, Green Gram, Chickpeas, Kidney Beans.

Uses: Dal, soups, curries, snacks.

Nutritional Value: Rich in protein, iron, and fiber.

D. Condiments and Spices

Examples: Cumin, Coriander, Mustard, Pepper, Cardamom, etc.

Uses: Add flavor, aroma, digestion, preservation.

4.1 History, Evolution and Growth of the Hotel Industry

The hotel industry evolved from basic shelter needs to luxury, technology-driven, and sustainable hospitality services.

Early Beginnings

  • Ancient Civilizations: Inns in Mesopotamia, Egypt, Greece, Rome. Guesthouses near thermal baths, Olympic venues.
  • Indias Tradition: Atithi Devo Bhava dharamshalas, serais, caravanserais for pilgrims and traders.
  • China: Government guesthouses along Silk Road.

Medieval Period (6th15th Century)

  • Monasteries offered free lodging.
  • Inns and taverns charged for food and stay.
  • Coaching inns in Europe for horse-drawn carriages.

Renaissance to Industrial Revolution (16th18th Century)

  • Growth in trade and exploration.
  • First luxury hotels: Hotel de Henry IV (Paris), City Hotel (1794, New York).

Industrial Revolution (18th19th Century)

  • Railways, steamships ? faster travel.
  • Tremont House (1829, Boston): Indoor plumbing, reception desk.
  • Savoy Hotel (1889, London): Electric lights, private bathrooms.

Modern Period (20th Century Onwards)

PeriodKey Features
Early 1900sGrand hotels (Ritz, Waldorf Astoria)
Post-WWIITourism expansion
1950s1970sHotel chains (Hilton, Sheraton)
1980s1990sBudget hotels, resorts
2000s onwardsGlobalization, online booking, eco-hotels

21st Century: Innovation & Sustainability

  • Digitalization: Booking.com, Airbnb, OYO
  • Smart Hotels: AI, IoT, contactless check-in
  • Sustainability: Green buildings, energy efficiency
  • New Segments: Capsule hotels, wellness resorts

Growth in India

  • Ancient: Dharamshalas
  • British Era: Great Eastern (1840), Taj Mahal Palace (1903)
  • Post-Independence: Taj, Oberoi, ITC
  • Modern: Marriott, Hyatt, Accor; focus on smart hotels, domestic tourism
