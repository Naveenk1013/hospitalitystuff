Introduction

Globally, providing a room with meals inclusive is a standard practice in the hospitality industry. Hotels, especially the upscale ones, often include meals in the room rate. A plan without meals is considered the cheapest, while those including full-course meals are priced higher.

Generally, there are four types of meal plans followed worldwide: American Plan (AP), Modified American Plan (MAP), Continental Plan (CP), and European Plan (EP).

What is a Meal Plan?

A meal plan is a package added to a room rate that includes meals for hotel guests. Choosing the right meal plan is essential for travelers to understand the inclusions and compare rates effectively.

Based on the type of meal plan, hotels are classified as follows:

  1. European Plan (EP)
  2. Continental Plan (CP)
  3. American Plan (AP)
  4. Modified American Plan (MAP)

1. European Plan (EP)

This plan includes only lodging, meaning the guest pays for accommodation only. Meals, beverages, and other services are charged separately. Guests are free to dine outside the hotel. This plan is commonly used by hotels in metropolitan cities and youth hostels.

Example: In India, most public sector hotels operate on the European Plan basis.

2. Continental Plan (CP)

Under this plan, the room rate includes lodging and breakfast. Guests are free to take other meals as per their choice, for which they are billed separately.

Example: A guest pays for bed and breakfast, and orders lunch or dinner as desired.

3. American Plan (AP)

Also known as the Full Board or Full Pension plan, the American Plan includes accommodation and three meals (breakfast, lunch, and dinner). It is popular in resorts or tourist destinations where guests prefer to dine within the hotel.

The menu is usually table d‚Äôh√¥te (a fixed meal with limited choices at a collective price).

Example: Hill resorts or beachside hotels offering all-inclusive packages.

4. Modified American Plan (MAP)

This plan includes lodging, breakfast, and one major meal‚Äîeither lunch or dinner. It offers flexibility to guests and is known as demi-pension.

Example: A business traveler may prefer breakfast and dinner included while managing lunch outside during meetings.

Topic: Tariff Fixation (Cost-Based & Market-Based)

Introduction to Tariff Fixation

In the hospitality industry, tariff refers to the price charged by a hotel for its rooms and services. Tariff fixation is a strategic process that considers both internal costs and external market dynamics to determine room pricing.

Importance of Tariff Fixation

  • Ensures profitability and sustainability
  • Helps in competitive positioning
  • Attracts the right market segment
  • Assists in revenue forecasting and budgeting

I. Cost-Based Tariff Fixation

Cost-based pricing involves setting room rates based on total operational costs plus a profit margin.

Formula: Tariff = Cost per room + Desired profit margin

Type of Cost Example
Fixed CostsSalaries, Rent, Property Tax, Insurance
Variable CostsUtilities, Housekeeping supplies, Guest amenities

Example ‚Äì Budget Hotel Calculation

  • Fixed cost: ‚Çπ9,00,000
  • Variable cost per occupied room: ‚Çπ300
  • Occupancy: 60%
  • Rooms sold: 540 per month
  • Total variable cost: ‚Çπ1,62,000
  • Total cost: ‚Çπ10,62,000
  • Cost per room: ‚Çπ1,966
  • Profit (20%): ‚Çπ393
  • Final Tariff: ‚Çπ2,360

Advantages of Cost-Based Tariff

  • Covers operational expenses
  • Ensures profitability
  • Easy to calculate

Limitations

  • Ignores market competition
  • May not attract price-sensitive guests

II. Market-Based Tariff Fixation

This method considers demand, competition, and customer perception to determine pricing. It is flexible and responsive to market conditions.

1. Competitor-Based Pricing

Hotels set tariffs similar to or slightly lower than nearby competitors to attract guests.

2. Demand-Based (Dynamic) Pricing

Rates change based on demand, season, or events. Example: A resort in Goa charging ‚Çπ8,000 during peak season and ‚Çπ3,500 in off-season.

3. Psychological Pricing

Prices are set to influence perception, e.g., ‚Çπ4,999 instead of ‚Çπ5,000.

4. Skimming Pricing

High rates for new luxury hotels to attract premium customers.

5. Penetration Pricing

Low introductory rates to enter a competitive market.

Example ‚Äì Market-Based Fixation (Star Hotel)

  • Marriott Mumbai weekday rate: ‚Çπ10,000
  • Weekend rate: ‚Çπ7,000
  • Event season rate: ‚Çπ14,000
  • Loyalty discount: 15%

Advantages

  • Aligns with market demand
  • Attracts guests during low occupancy
  • Encourages competitiveness

Limitations

  • Frequent rate changes may confuse guests
  • Low pricing can impact brand image

III. Hybrid Pricing Strategy

Most hotels use a mix of cost-based and market-based approaches. First, they calculate the minimum cost to avoid losses, then adjust prices according to demand and competitors.

Example: If the cost per room is ‚Çπ2,000 and the market rate is ‚Çπ3,000‚Äì‚Çπ3,500, the hotel may fix ‚Çπ3,200 to ensure profitability and competitiveness.

Unit 2: Arrival & Check-In Procedure

In the guest arrival stage there are certain key steps that ensure smooth service and a positive first impression.

Preparing for Guest Arrival

  • Verification of documents
  • Preparing movement list and expected arrival/departure lists

Movement List

It contains the names of guests expected to arrive and depart the next day.

Pre-Arrival Activities

  • Generate an arrivals list with estimated times, room types, and special requirements.
  • Prepare group arrivals, VIP, SPATT (Special Attention Guest), and handicapped guest lists.
  • Distribute relevant lists to Housekeeping, F&B, and Guest Relations departments.
  • Complete pre-registration for guests using information from reservations.
  • Prepare amenities vouchers for guests requesting special items at reservation time.

Preparation at Reception Desk

  • Conduct morning briefing and review the logbook.
  • Calculate expected arrivals and departures.
  • Determine room position:
    Room position = Available vacant rooms + expected departures ‚àí expected arrivals.
  • Allocate rooms for VIPs and other guests.

Welcoming or Receiving Guests

First impressions are vital. Reception staff must greet guests promptly, courteously, and with warmth, as this sets the tone for the entire stay.

Why Do Guests Need to Register?

  • Legal requirement for maintaining guest records.
  • Helps account for residents during emergencies.
  • Confirms acceptance of hotel terms and conditions.
  • Engages the guest while booking and room allocation are verified.

Registration Process

After arrival, the front desk agent completes registration to confirm accuracy and gather required details. Guests sign a printed registration card as a statutory requirement. Even with digital systems, a signed card may still be necessary.

Formats Used During Check-In

  1. Guest Registration Card (GRC):

    A vital document containing guest details like name, address, nationality, organization, arrival/departure dates, and room rate.

  2. C Form:

    A legal form filled by all foreign nationals (except Nepalese). Three copies are prepared‚Äîone for FRRO, one for the local police station, and one for hotel records.

  3. Arrival and Departure Register:

    Monitors daily arrivals and departures, helping housekeeping and front office track occupancy.

Assigning the Room and Rate

Room assignment involves allocating available rooms in specific categories based on reservation data. Rates and assignments are finalized at registration.

SOP ‚Äì Concierge / Bell Desk ‚Äì Guest Luggage Handling Procedure

On Guest Arrival

  • Retrieve luggage, confirm completeness, and tag it properly.
  • Escort the guest to reception and inform them you‚Äôll handle their luggage.

On Guest Departure

Luggage Storage Room

  • Ensure all luggage is tagged and recorded in the long-term storage register.
  • Mark fragile or perishable items appropriately and ensure safety and security.

Records and Procedures

1. Left Luggage Procedure

Guests can store luggage safely after check-out. The process includes:

  • Ensure bill settlement before accepting luggage.
  • Check for damage, paste luggage stickers, and issue a signed receipt.
  • Enter details in the left-luggage register and store securely.
  • Release only upon verifying receipt or signature in the register.

2. Scanty Baggage Procedure

Guests with minimal luggage are potential skippers (leave without payment). Controls include:

  • Notify lobby manager and reception on arrival.
  • Stamp ‚ÄúScanty Baggage‚Äù on arrival errand card and registration card copies.
  • Maintain a Scanty Baggage Register signed by the lobby manager.

Chapter: Check-out & Departure Procedure

3.1 Check-out & Departure Process

Definition: The Check-out and Departure process is the final phase of the guest cycle. It involves settling the guest‚Äôs bill, collecting keys, retrieving guest feedback, and bidding a courteous farewell. This stage plays a crucial role in leaving a lasting impression on the guest.

Steps in Check-out

  1. Guest approaches front desk for departure.
  2. Room status is verified with housekeeping.
  3. Mini-bar consumption and pending charges are confirmed.
  4. Bill is prepared and shown to the guest.
  5. Mode of payment is chosen and processed.
  6. Keys are collected and feedback form is offered.
  7. Receipt and thank-you note issued.
  8. Departure marked in PMS and room status updated.

Example: At The Oberoi, guests receive a digital bill summary on a tablet and provide real-time feedback before departing.

3.2 Modes of Settlement of Guest Bill

Hotels offer multiple methods to settle guest bills as per guest convenience and policy.

Mode Description Notes
Cash Guest pays in physical currency Must check for counterfeit notes
Foreign Currency Guest pays in internationally accepted currency Exchange rate applied as per RBI or hotel rate
Traveller‚Äôs Cheque (TC) Guest pays via pre-issued cheques Signature and ID proof mandatory; less common today
Travel Voucher Provided by tour operators or agencies Validated against contract; marked ‚ÄúBill to Company‚Äù
Bill to Company Bill sent to employer or sponsor Requires prior approval with credit terms

Example: A corporate guest from Siemens may check out using ‚ÄúBill to Company‚Äù with GST invoice sent later to their accounts department.

3.3 Credit Card Handling Procedure

Steps to Process Credit Card Payments

  1. Swipe or insert card into EDC machine or hotel POS system.
  2. Confirm guest name and last four digits.
  3. Enter amount and get OTP or signature.
  4. Match signature on slip with ID proof if required.
  5. Issue printed charge slip and hotel receipt.
  6. Update guest folio as Paid by Credit Card.

Best Practices

  • Do not store CVV or PIN.
  • Never process transactions without guest presence.
  • Use PCI-DSS compliant POS systems.

Real-life Tip: At Marriott, credit card slips are securely stored for 90 days to handle chargeback disputes.

3.4 Foreign Currency Exchanging Procedure (FC/TC)

  1. Confirm if hotel is an authorized money changer (AMC).
  2. Accept currency/TC within permitted RBI limits.
  3. Verify passport and visa for foreign nationals.
  4. Fill Foreign Exchange Encashment Certificate.
  5. Maintain currency exchange register and daily reports.

3.5 Collecting Keys & Guest Feedback

Key Collection Process

  • Ensure all keys/cards are returned.
  • Deactivate keycards in PMS.
  • Report lost keys to security and apply charges if applicable.

Guest Feedback Collection

  • Offer printed or digital feedback form.
  • Encourage guests to share quick comments or ratings.
  • Promote Google or TripAdvisor reviews.

Example: ITC Hotels use QR codes on check-out receipts for instant online feedback.

3.6 Final Settlement of Bill and Issuing Bill

  1. Check pending charges (laundry, minibar, spa, etc.).
  2. Apply discounts or loyalty points.
  3. Reconfirm guest identity and payment mode.
  4. Generate detailed invoice with GST.
  5. Guest signs copy for hotel record.
  6. Email or print a copy for the guest.

3.7 Left Luggage & Departure Procedure

Left Luggage Procedure

  1. Fill Left Luggage Tag and issue copy to guest.
  2. Tag bag with number and store securely.
  3. Record details in Left Luggage Register.
  4. Guest must show ID and receipt to reclaim.

Departure Final Steps

  • Bid farewell politely and express gratitude.
  • Offer transport or future booking assistance.
  • Mark room as vacant/dirty in PMS.

3.8 Control of Cash and Credit

Cash Control Measures

  • Maintain cashier shift reports.
  • Record every transaction with receipts.
  • Reconcile cash drawer at shift end.
  • Limit refunds or advances in cash.

Credit Control Measures

  • Grant credit only to pre-approved companies.
  • Maintain a credit ledger and aging report.
  • Follow up regularly with corporate accounts.
  • Pause credit for accounts overdue by more than 30 days.

Real-life Practice: At The Leela, front office verifies daily cash reports, countersigned by the night auditor and front office manager.

Conclusion

The Check-out & Departure phase marks the end of the guest cycle and plays a crucial role in guest satisfaction. A smooth and professional check-out process ensures lasting loyalty, operational accuracy, and a positive brand image.

Design Principles & Elements of Flower Arrangement

The art follows core design principles: balance, proportion, rhythm, variety, emphasis, harmony, repetition and distinction. Good design also considers background, container, scale and the relationship between components.

Key Principles (short list)

  • Scaling / Proportion
  • Balance: physical and visual
  • Rhythm (flow and motion)
  • Variety & Texture
  • Proper use of colour
  • Emphasis (focal point)
  • Harmony and distinction
  • Repetition (in moderation)

Design Guidelines

  • Stems should flow from a central point; avoid crossed stems
  • Focal point should draw the eye and may be the tallest element
  • Avoid monotonous flower choice; vary scale and opening stages
  • Shape must suit the container and site
  • No flower should be more than one-third the size of the container (general rule)
  • Height of material: about 1 to 1.5 times container height (or width for long low containers)

Location, Placement & Effect on Design

The site of an arrangement affects scale, fragrance choices and visibility.

Common Locations and Guidelines

  • Table arrangements: Low, unobtrusive, viewable from all sides; must not block conversation.
  • Reception desk: Bold, front-facing and dominant to suit large counters.
  • Guest rooms: Small, non-perfumed arrangements; size proportionate to room and surface.

Rule of thumb: Tall vases - tallest elements 1.5 to 2 times vase length. Lower arrangements require smaller stems to conform visually.

Plant Material: Types, Conditioning & Preparation

Plant Material Types

  • Dominant (focal): Gerbera, roses, lilies, dahlias, anthurium
  • Fillers (secondary): Asters, gypsophila, button chrysanthemums
  • Foliage (line): Ferns, palms, asparagus fern

Conditioning & Hardening

Proper conditioning prolongs life: plunge cut flowers into water immediately, cut stems on slant, harden (soak) for hours, strip foliage below waterline, use preservatives, and avoid direct drafts and sunlight.

Important Handling Rules

  • Cut stems at a slant with sharp tools (early morning or after sunset)
  • Carry cut flowers heads-down to avoid snapping
  • Re-cut stems under water to remove air bubbles and callus
  • Remove foliage below waterline to reduce bacterial growth
  • Use commercial preservatives or simple mixes (sugar + tiny bleach) to slow bacterial growth

Cutting, Grooming, Water Temperature & Placement Care

How to Cut & Re-cut

  • Always cut at a slant; re-cut stems that have been out of water
  • For woody stems: scrape or split ends to expose inner tissue
  • Hollow stems: plug with cotton and use as wick if needed
  • Milky/latex stems: briefly sear cut ends to stop sap leak

Water Temperature

Warm water aids uptake; brief boiling or searing may sterilize the end and expel air bubbles, improving water uptake for certain stems.

Grooming & Maintenance

  • Wash foliage, trim damaged leaves and dead flowers
  • Regularly change water, clean containers and add preservative
  • Misting morning and evening preserves freshness
  • Avoid placing arrangements near ripening fruit (ethylene gas) or direct heat sources

An effective uniform room layout should allow smooth flow from receipt ‚Üí storage ‚Üí issuing ‚Üí return/exchange while keeping clean and soiled uniforms separate.
